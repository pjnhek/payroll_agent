<objective>
Publish the phase's evidence where its intended audience will actually find it: a
`docs/DURABILITY-PROOFS.md` linked from the README, carrying one section per proof — claim,
mutation, pasted red, byte-identical revert green, exact re-run command — plus a companion section
stating the accepted residuals (D-07, D-08).

Purpose: "here is the mutation that breaks it, here is the red output" is the single most
differentiating artifact this milestone produces for its primary audience. Burying it in
`.planning/` would waste it. And publishing the boundary next to the claim is itself the
differentiator: a system that states precisely what it does *not* guarantee is making a stronger
claim about the rest, not a weaker one.

Output: the evidence document, the README link, a rot-guard test, and the human verification of the
one `/ops` property no automated test can judge.
</objective>

    1. **The claim** — what this proof establishes, in one or two sentences, in the design

    4. **The revert green** — confirmation that the mutation was reverted byte-identically and the
       proof passed again, with the commit SHA it all ran against.
    5. **Re-run it yourself** — the exact command, including the environment variables a live
       database requires. State plainly that these proofs need a real Postgres and self-skip
       without one, and that a skip is not a pass.

    it checks at the selection layer, why an execution-layer "N passed" guard cannot see a proof
    that was never selected, and the typo falsification plan 21-09 executed against it. A guard is
    also a claim, and it gets the same evidence treatment as the four proofs.

    Then the companion section — **what is not guaranteed** — stating these three residuals plainly
    and without hedging (D-08):

    - **Exactly-once delivery is not achievable.** This is the Two Generals problem, not a library
      gap. What is guaranteed is at most one confirmation per approved run, per epoch.
    - **Recovery is best-effort within roughly thirty minutes.** GitHub Actions cron can be delayed,
      and a scheduled workflow auto-disables after sixty days of repository inactivity. The stated
      fallback is operator retry, which is why the retrigger affordance exists.
    - **An operator retrigger can legitimately send a second email.** It bumps the reply epoch by
      design. That is why the claim is "at most once per approved run, per epoch" and not a flat
      "never twice."

    Close with a pointer to `/ops` as the operational counterpart: the proofs establish the
    guarantees, and the ops page is where an operator checks whether the system is currently inside
    them.

    Keep the document self-contained for a reader who has never seen `.planning/`. Do not reference
    plan numbers, wave numbers, or GSD artifacts in the published text.
